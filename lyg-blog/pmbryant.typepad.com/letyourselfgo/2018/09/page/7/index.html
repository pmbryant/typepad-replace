Summer Stock (1950)

From my Facebook archives, December 14, 2014:

Summer Stock 1950. This was Judy Garland's last musical at MGM, the studio where she had worked since 1935 when she was 13 years old. It's got Judy in great form (on screen at least), and Gene Kelly as well, so of course it's enjoyable. The plot is a bit tired and implausible, but fortunately the performances make it easy to forget that, behind the scenes, there was turmoil.

But if you are interested in that turmoil... http://www.tcm.com/this…/article/24094%7C0/Summer-Stock.html

This was the 13th of Judy's films that we've seen--all in the last year or so, except the Wizard of Oz (which of course we first saw many many years ago).

Let's Dance (1950)

From my Facebook archives, December 9, 2014:

We're down to our last few Fred Astaire musicals. 'Let's Dance' from 1950 is our 26th to see, leaving just four more.

This one has Betty Hutton as Fred's partner. She is her own thing, that's for sure, and is fascinating to watch. The music is good, if not exceptional. The story is serviceable for a musical (certainly better than Royal Wedding and especially Belle of New York, the last two of Fred's films we've seen). The acting is a bit weaker than usual, but not in any way that seriously hurts. The dancing routines were, of course, fabulous (except for the finale, which wasn't quite so memorable). Not Royal Wedding fabulous, but that's a very high standard.

From reading the book 'Music Makes Me: Fred Astaire and Jazz' earlier this year, I had been under the impression that, in one of his dance solos, Fred destroys a piano. And while Fred did indeed dance under, in, and on top of a piano, then played a tune on another one, he left them both intact in the end. I shouldn't have taken that author's phrase "smashing up the tools of his trade" quite so literally.

Anyway this was very fun to watch and hardly deserves to be "forgotten", as that same author describes it. This is the only one of Fred's films that doesn't appear to be available on DVD. Fortunately it is on Amazon Prime streaming and watchable by taking their 30-day free trial.

Lucky Partners (1940)

From my Facebook archives, December 2, 2014:
 
Lucky Partners is the movie I posted the still from a couple nights ago (or 'Double Chance' in the French poster below). It's an extremely charming 1940 film starring Ginger Rogers and Ronald Colman. I am into comedies from the '30s and '40s and this is up there with the best of them. It started fun and just got better and better as it went along.

I've seen many of Ginger's films by now, but this was the first time I recall seeing Ronald Colman, though he was also a big star. (Of course, until last year I hadn't seen any of Ginger Rogers' films either.) Colman generally did more serious fare it seems, and we just don't watch dramas nearly as much as comedies. I also wasn't familiar with the director, Lewis Milestone, but he too was apparently very accomplished at dramas, including 'All Quiet on the Western Front' (which I haven't seen yet but really should). Jack Carson and Spring Byington in supporting roles are more familiar faces to me -- and they are always welcome.

State Fair (1945)

From my Facebook archives, November 29, 2014:

I don't remember how our latest old Hollywood musical, State Fair from 1945, ended up in our [Netflix DVD] queue. None of our musical 'regulars' were involved with it: Fred Astaire, Ginger Rogers, Judy Garland, Busby Berkeley, Hermes Pan, Betty Grable. The Story of Vernon and Irene Castle (1939)

From my Facebook archives, November 23, 2014:

"The Story of Vernon and Irene Castle" starring Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers, released in 1939.

We watched this tonight, the latest in our second pass through Fred and Ginger's movies. It is the ninth and final one they made during their legendary run at RKO in the 1930s. In many ways, it is the most unusual of their films together, as they are portraying a real-life dance team and doing dances in the style of the Castles rather than their own. It is also a far more serious story than their usual musical comedy fare.

I still love it, of course!

From Arlene Croce's classic book on Fred and Ginger:

"It was a mating of subject and stars such as movies seldom see, and it was made at a perhaps unguessably right moment in time for both the subject and the stars. It is a very dear film."

Footlight Parade (1933)

From my Facebook archives, November 22, 2014:

The 1933 Busby Berkeley musical spectacular 'Footlight Parade' was our entertainment last night. This was our second watching and it was just as impressive the second time around. The plot and acting by James Cagney (his first movie musical) and Joan Blondell and the rest of the cast keep it interesting until the final act, when three back-to-back-to-back spectacular Busby Berkeley productions steal the show. Truly a classic film.

1776 (1972)

From my Facebook archives, November 15, 2014:

'1776' is the latest film we watched, finally finishing it last night. This was made in the early '70s so is pretty modern by our standards. Mostly entertaining and quite funny in places, but the music is not its strength. And that seems like a major problem in a musical.

The fellow who played Benjamin Franklin was my favorite. And it was a trip seeing a young John Cullum, who I know so well as Holling on Northern Exposure. His song was the best of the musical numbers as well.
