Young Man of Manhattan (1930): Ginger Rogers' first movie

From my Facebook archives, April 22, 2015:

Last week we watched a Paramount film from 1930 that may well be the oldest movie I've yet seen in full (as I haven't yet gotten into silents). It was called Young Man of Manhattan and starred Claudette Colbert and Norman Foster.

For me, it is notable as it marked the on-screen debut of Ginger Rogers--who at age 18 was playing a rich 16-year old flapper with a Helen Kane/Betty Boop-like voice. It's actually a rather large role for a newcomer. She plays an important part in the plot, gets to sing a couple short songs (and this isn't even a musical), and has a line that apparently became a catch-phrase of that era: "Cigarette me, big boy!" It's a trip seeing her so early in her career, with a rather different appearance from when she became a huge star four years later, after about twenty more films.

This film itself is quite enjoyable, as a drama about the relationship between a talented, ambitious woman reporter (Colbert) and her insecure husband and colleague (Foster). These two were married in real life at the time.

Ginger is certainly fun to watch in this film, though I do think her character is meant to be somewhat annoying. She plays the one who gets 'in the way' of the happy marriage.

The sound quality of the print I have is not as good as films I've seen from 1933 on. I'm not sure if that's because this was so much earlier in sound movie history (3 years was a lot at that time) or because it was a lower budget film or because it needs some restoration work. But it was quite watchable fortunately.

Stage Door Canteen (1943)

From my Facebook archives, April 19, 2015:

Continuing to get caught up on films we've seen over the last two weeks...

Stage Door Canteen. United Artists, 1943. This film, made in the middle of World War 2, is pure propaganda and morale boosting entertainment. Dozens of actors and musicians appear in cameos, with some performing more substantial bits. The majority were people I am not familiar with, but fortunately most were identified. The movie was fascinating to watch, but mainly for a couple of the musical performances (Benny Goodman, in particular) and as a cultural study of America at war.

Wife Versus Secretary (1936)

From my Facebook archives, April 18, 2015:

Despite the corny poster here and comedic title, Wife Versus Secretary (MGM, 1936) is an excellent dramatic film. Can you guess who between Myrna Loy and Jean Harlow played 'Wife' and who played 'Secretary'?

All the main performers are excellent, but for me, Jean Harlow particularly stood out. This surprised me as I haven't been a big fan of her before. She was good in the fabulous ensemble drama Dinner at Eight, but I wasn't impressed by her in Public Enemy. Here, she was fantastic and turned this film from good entertainment into a memorable and classic drama.

Jimmy Stewart had a significant supporting role as Harlow's grumpy boyfriend, but clearly was not yet considered a star as he is not given any significant billing.

Myrna Loy (as 'Wife' of course) is, as always, great fun to watch.

The Devil and Miss Jones (1941)

From my Facebook archives, April 14, 2015:

The Devil and Miss Jones. RKO, 1941.

Perhaps not so surprising, given that it stars Jean Arthur and Charles Coburn, this is an excellent comedy. These same two did the fabulous The More The Merrier (with Joel McCrea) a couple years later

The story here is actually very intriguing, concerning an ultra-wealthy capitalist going undercover to personally attempt to weed out labor agitators. He is the 'devil' of the title. But, played by Charles Coburn, he comes to sympathize with the concerns of the workers against the executives. These executives are ruthless in their attempts to quash any hint of labor organizing. This realistic touch added a lot to the film for me.

Jean Arthur is, as usual, lovably, amusingly charming in her role as Coburn's "mentor", while he poses as a new employee.

The ending is a bit abrupt and not very satisfying to me. But 98% of this film is fantastic.

Kitty Foyle (1940)

From my Facebook archives, April 4, 2015:

Kitty Foyle. RKO, 1940. This is the film Ginger Rogers won her Academy award for. I've put off seeing it longer than I should have, perhaps because it's a serious drama while I have been mainly focused on comedies and fun musicals.

We finally watched it last week and it's an excellent movie all-around. Ginger dominates the picture and of course is superb. Her two male leads are also very good--Dennis Morgan and James Craig--neither of whom I'd been familiar with before. I particularly liked Craig and the amusing scenes where he first meets and "dates" Ginger-as-Kitty Foyle, spending the evening playing cards in the cramped apartment she shares with two other women.

Ginger's scene where she describes her expectant child as her "candidate for the year 2000" is haunting.

The only issue I have with the film is that it could have been a bit longer. This is an "epic" storyline that takes place over a number of years and involves plenty of twists and turns. I wouldn't have minded it being spread out over a longer playing time than its actual 108 minutes. (Of course that's easy for me to say watching from comfort of my own home with flexibility to shut it off and pick up again the next night.)

This film, as with so many of Ginger's movies, deserves to be more widely remembered today.

Silk Stockings (1957)

From my Facebook archives, March 30, 2015:

Silk Stockings. MGM, 1957.

This was Fred Astaire's last "classic" Hollywood musical, at the age of 58. He did a number of films after this, but all non-singing and non-dancing, except for one -- 11 years later. His dancing shifted to television.

The dancing here, except for the first group routine, is really superb. Fred is still great, although no longer in his prime. Cyd Charisse is excellent.

Yet I did not warm up to the film. The Soviet stereotypes are overdone. The characters of the three commissars are more tiresome than funny (except for Peter Lorre). Fred's character is a bit of a sleaze. Cyd Charisse's character is flat. The romance isn't believable.

And worst of all was the demeaning attitude towards women, verbalized most egregiously in this lyric that Cyd Charisse sings (dubbed) to Fred while kneeling on the floor in front of him: "A woman to a man is just a woman. But a man to a woman is her life."

So the two romantic duets between Fred and Cyd, while excellent routines, suffer from the context they are put in. That is a critical difference from his earlier films.

The most enjoyable bits were the Cyd-plus-chorus number 'Red Blues' and Fred's very funny 'sock solo' (as he called it) "The Ritz Roll and Rock". Both of these are in the last part of the film so it does end on a strong note.

Three Smart Girls (1936)

From my Facebook archives, March 26, 2015:

Three Smart Girls. Universal, 1936.

Jeanine Basinger's book The Star Machine has led me to check out films by a few once-upon-a-time big, but not well-known today, stars I haven't been familiar with yet. Such as Tyrone Power in Alexander's Ragtime Band and now Deanna Durbin in this movie, her first (at the age of 15).

Durbin is a singing actress like Judy Garland, except with a very different style (more operatic, less jazz) that is generally not to our taste.

In this film, the singing is minimal, and it is mostly a crazy comedy. And it is a very good comedy at that, so it's not hard to see why it was a big hit. The supporting cast is excellent. Alice Brady is a particular favorite of mind thanks to her work in The Gay Divorcee with Fred and Ginger.

Alexander's Ragtime Band (1938)

From my Facebook archives, March 21, 2015:

I've fallen a bit behind in my movie blogging. I have three excellent films to report, all musicals to varying degrees, all from the 1930s.

Alexander's Ragtime Band. 20th Century Fox. 1938.

Alice Faye! How had I never seen one of her films before? That has to change. Don Ameche is also excellent, but he is always one of my favorites. And Tyrone Power is pretty good too.

There are a ton of Irving Berlin songs here (29 reportedly), as it appears to have been the first of a number of Berlin songfests, including the Fred Astaire films Holiday Inn, Blue Skies, and Easter Parade from 5-10 years later. Fortunately these are good songs and the performers are wonderful so it hardly feels like a re-run. Which would be unfair anyway as this movie came first.

Theodora Goes Wild (1936)

From my Facebook archives, March 14, 2015:

Theodora Goes Wild, a 1936 romantic comedy starring Irene Dunne and Melvyn Douglas, was disappointing overall. That was surprising to me because so many seem to really like this film, and I'm pretty forgiving of comedies. The main problem was the story. Douglas' character wins over Irene Dunne by stalking her and blackmailing her and being extremely rude and obnoxious to all around in the process. Then she re-pays the favor. The dog was cute though.

Daddy Long Legs (1955)

From my Facebook archives, March 8, 2015:

Daddy Long Legs. 20th Century Fox, 1955. This film will always hold a special distinction for us, since it was where we first discovered the magic of Fred Astaire back in September 2013. We watched it for the second time a couple weeks ago. In the interim, I've gone from completely unknowledgeable about Fred's life and career to a sort of "expert", having seen all his musical films except the final two, and having read numerous books about him. It was interesting seeing Daddy Long Legs with this new perspective.

For example, I now know the Fred's wife had died from lung cancer very shortly before the filming was to begin, and that Fred has tried to get out of making the picture, but had been talked into continuing by his friends and colleagues. The producers arranged the schedule to accommodate Fred's grief, and in the final product his pain is undetectable. It is a fun, upbeat film and his performance on screen fits that mood perfectly.

On a more technical note, this was the first widescreen ("CinemaScope") film that Fred had done. This wasn't noticeable on our first viewing, as widescreen films are so prevalent to modern eyes. But after watching all his earlier films in approximate chronological order, the sudden jump to widescreen is now quite apparent to me.

Also it's clear that Daddy Long Legs is one of the best of Fred's later musicals.I'm glad this was the first film of his we saw rather than either of the two that came immediately before and after, The Band Wagon and Funny Face. Those are good, but don't show off Fred's magic anywhere near its best. We would not have gotten the right impression of Fred from watching those.

For that fortunate occurrence, we can thank Netflix's streaming service. We had just decided to start watching some old classic musicals. After seeing Gene Kelly in An American in Paris, we wanted to see another dance movie. But we wanted to see a different star. Fred Astaire was the logical choice. Netflix's only streaming option for us that night was Daddy Long Legs. (Now they also have Funny Face available.)

We were novices, but still knew enough to realize this was late-career Fred and probably not his prime work. Still, he won us over immediately with his drumming and dance solo five minutes into the film. Drums are a quick way to both of our hearts. Discovering Fred was a drummer as well as a dancer was a revelation.

As for the plot of the film, with the protagonists (Fred and Leslie Caron) so far apart in age, it could easily have become creepy. But fortunately, due to the approach taken by the writers, it never did. It's an entertaining film with lots of great dancing--though the film-makers could have cut the long "dream ballet" by Caron near the end without losing anything memorable.
