Honor Among Lovers (1931): Ginger Rogers in her smallest role

From my Facebook archives, July 31, 2015:

Honor Among Lovers (1931) was the fifth of Ginger Rogers' 73 films and the one with her smallest role. But it is a very entertaining film nonetheless, starring Claudette Colbert and Fredric March and directed by Dorothy Arzner.

I just watched it tonight for the first time--on a copy with very deteriorated video quality such that it was frequently hard to make out the actors' faces.

Given that it's a very good film, I was happy to discover that it has apparently been restored by UCLA and is being shown there in September as part of an Arzner retrospective.

I've found older news items about similar Arzner retrospectives in other locations, so hopefully we can get the chance to see the restored version ourselves at some point.

Honor Among Lovers / Merrily We Go To Hell -- UCLA Film & Television Archive

-----

Update in September 2018: Good news! Universal, which owns the old Paramount library, release a restored version of this film on DVD earlier this summer.

Lillian Russell (1940)

From my Facebook archives, July 19, 2015:

Lillian Russell. 20th Century Fox, 1940. A few weeks back we finally got around to seeing another of Alice Faye's musicals (after seeing the excellent Alexander's Ragtime Band a few months ago).

The film Lillian Russell s a bio-pic of a superstar actress of the turn of the last century. Alas we don't get a good sense of Russell as a performer in this film. Alice Faye does sing in elaborate 1890s outfits, but apparently the actual Lillian Russell did a lot more than sing. The story is also extremely fictionalized so you don't get a good sense of Russell as a person either. After reading about her, I discovered that her actual story was far more interesting than the screenplay of this film.

What is interesting in the movie is seeing the nostalgia for the late 1800s, a period which so many then alive remembered very well, and seeing the negative portrayal of Russell's suffragette-activist mother. Also, there are a couple good songs. My favorite is one Russell's composer-husband, played by Don Ameche (who is underutilized in this film), sings to Alice Faye, "Blue Lovebird." While many of the songs are from the appropriate era, that was a new song written for the movie.

Henry Fonda is also in the film and plays an important, but boring, character. Edward Arnold and Warren William have smaller roles and are far more fun to watch.

Professional Sweetheart (1933): Early Ginger Rogers in a starring role

From my Facebook archives, July 8, 2015:

Professional Sweetheart. RKO, 1933. A young Ginger Rogers stars in this comedy about a star radio singer trying to break out of the confines of her image as the 'Purity Girl'.

There are several excellent actors and comedians in supporting roles in this film--notably Frank McHugh, Zasu Pitts, and Franklin Pangborn. And they all all great. Yet Ginger dominates the film in the role of the spoiled, manipulative, overgrown adolescent celebrity radio singer.

Alas, for those of us who thoroughly enjoy Ginger's singing, her voice is dubbed by someone with a very different style (Etta Moten). This is fortunately the only film where that was done.

The vast majority of the film is extremely funny and entertaining. Alas, the last few minutes don't make much sense. Ginger's character undergoes a sudden personality transplant. The script suggests that she suddenly becomes a "good woman" after getting punched in the face by her new husband (played by Norman Foster).

But the film up until then is so fun that the off-kilter finale can't spoil it.

Perfect Strangers (1950): Ginger Rogers' romantic drama in a courtroom

From my Facebook archives, June 29, 2015:

Perfect Strangers. Warner Brothers, 1950. Ginger Rogers stars with Dennis Morgan in this drama about a clandestine romance between two jury members who are married to other people. Morgan was also Ginger's love interest in her Academy Award-winning performance in Kitty Foyle a decade earlier.

The supporting cast includes Thelma Ritter and Alan Reed. I don't know Alan Reed as an actor aside from this film, but he later was the voice of Fred Flintstone. The director has the fabulous name of Bretaigne Windust. He is most well-known for his Broadway work in the 1940s.

I really enjoyed the complicated dynamics of the love story between Ginger and Dennis Morgan. The film also focused on the interpersonal dynamics of a sequestered jury in a highly-publicized murder case, which leads to a number of very amusing situations. The character played Anthony Ross, who keeps hitting on Ginger unsuccessfully, was particularly entertaining. The courtroom protocol stuff could have been cut down substantially.

After seeing this movie a few weeks ago, I read reviews on IMDB and was surprised at how unenthusiastic many were. Much of the negativity concerned the love story allegedly distracting from the courtroom story. I find that interpretation backwards. The love story *is* the movie. The rest is amusing and moderately interesting, but just background.

So if you want a courtroom drama uninterrupted by messy romance, this movie isn't for you. But if you care to watch a romantic drama that just happens to be set in a courtroom, then you may well enjoy this film after all!

Jezebel (1938)

From my Facebook archives, June 17, 2015:

There are three films we've seen over the last couple weeks that I still need to write about. All are quite enjoyable to varying degrees. First, Finian's Rainbow, Fred Astaire's last major musical; second, Perfect Strangers, a 1950 jury room romance drama starring Ginger Rogers; and most recently, the one I am writing about today, Jezebel, a 1938 Warner Brothers drama set in the plantation South, directed by William Wyler, and with Bette Davis is the title role.

Jezebel was made the year before Gone With The Wind was released, but is in the same genre (albeit in black-and-white and not so lengthy). It's a spectacularly-made film, with fabulous sets and costumes.

As may be expected from a 1930s film set in the South, black slaves are portrayed in the stereotyped manner, which is hard to watch at times. A few of the black cast do have meatier roles than in most films of this era, notably Lou Payton as Uncle Cato.

For me the film started slow, but it gradually became more and more compelling as it went along, until it became extremely dramatic in the final third. The final scenes are superb and well worth everything preceding.

Bette Davis dominates the film and understandably won an Oscar for her role. Henry Fonda and George Brent do well, if not spectacularly, in their roles.

Fay Bainter won an Oscar for her supporting role, though frankly I did not notice her character on this first viewing as much as perhaps I should have.

The Plainsman (1936)

From my Facebook archives, June 3, 2015:

The Plainsman is a Paramount western from 1936, directed by Cecil B. DeMille, starring Gary Cooper as Wild Bill Hickok, Jean Arthur as Calamity Jane, and James Ellison as Buffalo Bill Cody.

Alas, I don't think it was a very good film overall. The storyline has only the flimsiest basis in reality despite using the names of real people as characters. But more problematic was the romanticized view of the violence of that time period and the over-the-top mythologizing about the (white) pioneers who "built the country" at the expense of American Indians.

Hickok is portrayed here as a murderous thug, yet is supposed to be the hero. There are some minor pleadings to cease the violence but the characters concerned about that soon vanish from the screen.

Even Jean Arthur can't save this, though the best parts are when she is present.

-----

In response to a comment at the time, I added:

"I guess I haven't seen too many old westerns yet and the ones I have seen present a more complicated, less mythologizing view: High Noon and Shane, in particular, though it's been a few years since I've seen those.

The movie was a bit odd, as sometimes it seems about to veer away from the myth-making. But those moments are fleeting. And then the final scene of the dead Custer and dead Hickok galloping along at the head of an army, with the "patriotic" caption... Well, that was a bit much for me."

Too Many Husbands (1940)

From my Facebook archives, May 31, 2015:

Too Many Husbands. Columbia 1940. Starring Jean Arthur, it should not be too shocking to find this another fun, charming comedy and very enjoyable. Melvyn Douglas and Fred MacMurray are the two husbands vying for her affection. The three stars are all excellent and play off one another wonderfully.

In an interesting coincidence, the director of Too Many Husbands, Wesley Ruggles, happens to be the brother of Charles Ruggles, who co-starred with Ginger Rogers in Queen High, the movie we watched immediately before this one. I hadn't been familiar with Wesley Ruggles before. His most notable film was perhaps Cimarron (1931), the first western to win the Academy Award for best picture.

Queen High (1930): Very early Ginger Rogers

From my Facebook archives, May 23, 2015:

When she was still a teenager and working on Broadway in Top Speed and Girl Crazy, Ginger Rogers made five films at Paramount's Astoria studios on Long Island. Young Man of Manhattan, which I wrote about a few weeks ago, was the first. Queen High, which we just watched this week, was the second. (Some sources say it's the third.)

Ginger is the female lead and gets to sing and dance a little, but the real stars are Charles Ruggles and Frank Morgan, who are very amusing actors. (Morgan would become immortalized nine years later as the Wizard of Oz.)

Ginger's character, once again with a high-pitched voice, though not so exaggerated as in Young Man, is referred to early on by Ruggles as a "half-baked flapper." But the plot reveals her to be far more baked than that as she works to get her way this time around, unlike in the earlier film.

The film starts off great. It gets erratic in the second half, though, when some of the comedy gets a bit mean-spirited. Still, it is a fun movie -- primarily a comedic farce with some short musical bits thrown in. The copy I have suffers a bit in quality, but was still watchable.

The Fabulous Dorseys (1947)

From my Facebook archives, May 17, 2015:

How about a bio-pic where the subjects play their own characters, acting out a broadly accurate, yet fictionalized version of their careers? That's The Fabulous Dorseys, a United Artists picture from 1947.

So don't watch this for the acting quality, but do watch for some excellent music from the swing era -- which I can't get enough of lately.

Tommy and Jimmy Dorsey perform, of course, but also you can see Art Tatum, Charlie Barnet, Helen O'Connell and others.

Interestingly, Frank Sinatra spent a couple of his early years singing with Tommy Dorsey's band but he is nowhere to be seen or heard from in this film.

On The Town (1948)

From my Facebook archives, May 17, 2015:
 
On the Town. M-G-M, 1949. This musical has in its cast Gene Kelly, Frank Sinatra, Vera-Ellen, Ann Miller, and Betty Garrett. With names like these, and its status as a "classic", I was expecting a better movie.

It was modestly enjoyable. But, looking back at it, the music is forgettable, the dancing is not very interesting, the acting is weak, and the storyline is not compelling.

Most of the numbers are group pieces, so Sinatra's singing and Kelly's dancing are watered down by the weaker singers and dancers in the ensemble. And Ann Miller should have had the lead female role. She is far more charismatic than Vera-Ellen and deserved more screen time.

Some aspects of the film I particularly enjoyed were the color location shots of New York City in 1949, Ann Miller's tap dancing, seeing Betty Garrett in her younger days (after originally knowing her as a recurring character on All in the Family), the characterization of the women as the aggressive, wise ones while the men were naive, the gender-stereotype-reversed scene where taxi driver Betty Garrett is strongly hitting on a reluctant Frank Sinatra.

It seems to be a repeatable pattern that I am disappointed at the legendary Freed unit MGM musicals from the late '40s and early '50s. Many love them and sing their praises loudly, but these movies don't seem to suit my musical film tastes well. Of course Easter Parade is an exception!
