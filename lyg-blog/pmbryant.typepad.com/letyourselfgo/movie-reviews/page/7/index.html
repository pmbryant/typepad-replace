Hollywood Hotel (1938)

From my Facebook archives, December 28, 2014:

Hollywood Hotel 1938. After the most recent film from our Busby Berkeley set, Varsity Show, I wasn't expecting too much out of this one. But it turned out to be a blast. The story (a Hollywood satire) was great fun for me right now. And the performances were just right (well, except for Hugh Herbert).

But mainly, it was the music! The music was great all around and even featured Benny Goodman's band in several spectacular pieces.

Don't expect to see any psychedelic kaleidoscopic performance numbers though. Berkeley seems to have moved past that by this time.

---

From a 2018 perspective, I find it interesting that I did not even note Glenda Farrell's role in this film at the time I watched it, even though I know I had "discovered" her earlier in 2014 when watching "Gold Diggers of 1935". Her role here must have been much less noticeable. Alas, I have not re-watched this film yet, but indeed I cannot recall Glenda in this film.

I Love You Again (1940)

From my Facebook archives, December 26, 2014:

We saw this 1940 comedy a few days ago: I Love You Again. I believe William Powell and Myrna Loy did some insane number of movies together, like 14 or so. This the fourth for us. Though Loy's part isn't very large and she doesn't have very much to do. Frank McHugh's part is more involved in the comedy. So it's really William Powell's movie, but that's never a bad thing.

---

In a comment, I added: "William Powell was the one of the first of the classic Hollywood actors/actresses who really drew me in a few years ago. My Man Godfrey is the film that did it (with an assist from The Thin Man)."

On a separate topic, I also wrote: "I haven't seen Libeled Lady in a while but remember enjoying it. I had no clue Jean Harlow was in it but didn't really know who she was at the time. The only movie or hers we've seen that I recall her performance was Dinner At Eight, which was excellent."

Almost four years have passed and I still have yet to re-watch My Man Godfrey or Libeled Lady, so I should get busy with that!

The Barkleys of Broadway (1949)

From my Facebook archives, December 23, 2014:

We re-watched The Barkleys of Broadway over the weekend. This was the tenth and final film Ginger Rogers and Fred Astaire made together. The first nine were all done at RKO from 1933 to 1939. Barkleys was done at MGM in 1949.

Since first discovering Fred & Ginger just over a year ago, we've now seen all their movies at least twice--except for the first, Flying Down to Rio (which isn't really a 'Fred & Ginger' movie, though they steal the show).

Barkleys is rather different from most of their movies. It's the only one in color. It's the only MGM musical (and the MGM style is quite apparent). It was originally designed for Fred and Judy Garland as a follow up to Easter Parade. And it's a decade after Fred & Ginger's last collaboration.

Many parts of this film are magical. A few don't work for me. Overall it is a delightful film. You can't help but feel good after watching these two.

Talk of the Town (1942)

From my Facebook archives, December 18, 2014:

Talk of the Town 1942.

After we saw the Ginger Rogers-Ronald Colman film Lucky Partners a couple weeks back, I decided I should see more of Colman. And since Leslie described Talk of the Town as excellent, it seemed a good choice. And indeed it was! (Thanks Leslie!) I'd also been meaning to see more of Jean Arthur, so that was a bonus.

The film is hard to classify, as it wants to make serious points about the nature of justice in this country, yet is also a romantic comedy with a love triangle. Overall it works very well. With Colman, Arthur, and Cary Grant, and directed by George Stevens, that can't be surprising.

Primrose Path (1940)

From my Facebook archives, December 17, 2014:

Primrose Path 1940. Ginger Rogers and Joel McCrea. Three word review: Very Highly Recommended!

This is relatively unusual amongst the 26 films of Ginger Rogers that we've seen so far in being a drama rather than a comedy. And it continues the unbroken string of highly enjoyable films of hers. Why her non-Astaire movies aren't more widely known and appreciated, I cannot say. (Is the fact that it's hard to get very many of them through Netflix a cause or a symptom?)

Ginger did Primrose Path a year after her ninth and last RKO pairing with Fred Astaire. And it was for this role, as a young girl from a poor family, that she decided to die her hair dark, after becoming so famous as a glamorous blonde. Then she went even darker for Lucky Partners, immediately afterwards--which was our previous Ginger film.

The supporting cast in this film is excellent. Particularly noteworthy to me were Henry Travers (the guardian angel from It's a Wonderful Life), who had many amusing lines, and Queenie Vassar, who nearly stole the show in her role as the mean grandma. And this was apparently her first film appearance--at 70 years old!

The scenes in her family's small house are more "play-like" then typical in films. That setting dominates the latter half of the film. The first half seems more open, with the location shots of the shantytown and the road and the beach, in particular.

Gregory LaCava is a fascinating director. I think that's the fourth film of his I've seen: My Man Godfrey, Stage Door, 5th Ave Girl, Primrose Path. All excellent, but each different.

Let's Dance (1950)

From my Facebook archives, December 9, 2014:

We're down to our last few Fred Astaire musicals. 'Let's Dance' from 1950 is our 26th to see, leaving just four more.

This one has Betty Hutton as Fred's partner. She is her own thing, that's for sure, and is fascinating to watch. The music is good, if not exceptional. The story is serviceable for a musical (certainly better than Royal Wedding and especially Belle of New York, the last two of Fred's films we've seen). The acting is a bit weaker than usual, but not in any way that seriously hurts. The dancing routines were, of course, fabulous (except for the finale, which wasn't quite so memorable). Not Royal Wedding fabulous, but that's a very high standard.

From reading the book 'Music Makes Me: Fred Astaire and Jazz' earlier this year, I had been under the impression that, in one of his dance solos, Fred destroys a piano. And while Fred did indeed dance under, in, and on top of a piano, then played a tune on another one, he left them both intact in the end. I shouldn't have taken that author's phrase "smashing up the tools of his trade" quite so literally.

Anyway this was very fun to watch and hardly deserves to be "forgotten", as that same author describes it. This is the only one of Fred's films that doesn't appear to be available on DVD. Fortunately it is on Amazon Prime streaming and watchable by taking their 30-day free trial.

Lucky Partners (1940)

From my Facebook archives, December 2, 2014:
 
Lucky Partners is the movie I posted the still from a couple nights ago (or 'Double Chance' in the French poster below). It's an extremely charming 1940 film starring Ginger Rogers and Ronald Colman. I am into comedies from the '30s and '40s and this is up there with the best of them. It started fun and just got better and better as it went along.

I've seen many of Ginger's films by now, but this was the first time I recall seeing Ronald Colman, though he was also a big star. (Of course, until last year I hadn't seen any of Ginger Rogers' films either.) Colman generally did more serious fare it seems, and we just don't watch dramas nearly as much as comedies. I also wasn't familiar with the director, Lewis Milestone, but he too was apparently very accomplished at dramas, including 'All Quiet on the Western Front' (which I haven't seen yet but really should). Jack Carson and Spring Byington in supporting roles are more familiar faces to me -- and they are always welcome.

State Fair (1945)

From my Facebook archives, November 29, 2014:

I don't remember how our latest old Hollywood musical, State Fair from 1945, ended up in our [Netflix DVD] queue. None of our musical 'regulars' were involved with it: Fred Astaire, Ginger Rogers, Judy Garland, Busby Berkeley, Hermes Pan, Betty Grable. But it showed up in the mail so we gave it a spin last night. It was fun, light, not particularly memorable except for the giant prize-winning boar Blue Boy (big pigs always remind us of our dog [Ginger]!).
