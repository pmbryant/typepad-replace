December 13, 2006

Congratulations to our new congressman and all his supporters

Ciro Rodriguez, the voters of the 23rd congressional district of Texas, and everyone who helped out won a great victory yesterday.  And with that win, Democrats knock off Henry Bonilla, who went in one week from labelling Rodriguez as a terrorist-supporter, to reportedly making a gracious concession call, where he offered to work with Rodriguez during the transition.

We went down to the victory rally at the packed Harlandale Civic Center on the south side last night, expecting possibly a long evening.  As it turns out, though, the margin was larger than anyone's highest expectations, and we were there barely an hour before the race was called and the candidate had made his triumphant entrance and victory speech, proclaiming that he would be very proud to help make history by casting a vote for the first female Speaker of the House.

If we'd been reading Burnt Orange Report, the night may have been even shorter, since their expert knowledge of the voting returns across the district allowed them to call the race twenty minutes or so ahead of the AP.

To continue my brief foray into the world of YouTube, I have a couple minute segment of Ciro's victory speech last night. 

Also, my wife and partner here, while out on campaign trail yesterday, decided to become an amateur reporter for a day and captured a couple brief interviews with congressman Joe Baca of California and former congressional candidate John Courage, both of whom had joined Rodriguez on the final GOTV effort yesterday.  Here they are answering the simple question, "Why are you here today campaigning for Ciro Rodriguez?"

First, Rep. Joe Baca, who represents the San Bernardino area, a few dozen miles inland from Los Angeles in southern California.

Next, John Courage, who unfortunately lost his race to GOP Rep. Lamar Smith in November.  (His district stretches from northern San Antonio to the Hill Country to the Austin area.  The heavily conservative area around Kerrville that the Supreme Court removed from Bonilla and Rodriguez's district was added to Smith and Courage's district, making Courage's uphill run against Smith much tougher than it was originally.)

I doubt we'll be doing much more video here at B and B in the near future, but these snippets will serve as mementos of an amazing campaign that we were fortunate to play a small role in. Congratulations to congressman-elect Ciro Rodriguez and to all of his supporters!

Vote Ciro!

A couple weeks ago, when I first went down to Ciro Rodriguez's newly-organized office on the northwest side of town, we would have been extremely happy to go into this runoff election day in what is generally considered a dead heat.  But then again, that just makes waiting for the final results all the more nerve-wracking.

I am feeling optimistic.  In November, when Bonilla was running against seven opponents, but dominated the local media landscape, Bonilla still could not get to 50%.  Now, with the anti-Bonilla vote concentrated on a single candidate, and Democrats' financial situation much improved, and, of course, all the energy and publicity surrounding Bill Clinton's visit just a couple days ago, it's hard to imagine Bonilla doing much better.  On the other hand, November was a regular election that everyone knew about, and there were tons of other races to draw people out to the polls, while this runoff is a peculiar December election scheduled on a religious holiday celebrated by a large number of the people in this district, and there is only a single race on the ballot, albeit a race that has gotten quite a bit of attention in recent days.

Momentum, if there is such a thing, seems to be on Ciro's side, as Jaime Castillo wrote in the Express-News yesterday.  There are just a few more hours to make it happen.

December 11, 2006

For once, in Texas, our votes are meaningful

The big day approaches rapidly in Texas Congressional District 23. Tomorrow voters from San Antonio to El Paso will decide if Henry Bonilla will get another term to continue furthering the failed Bush/DeLay agenda, or whether Ciro Rodriguez will join the new Democratic congressional majority to pursue a new course in the interests of the people of this country.

Anyone in the Bexar County portion of the district who doesn't know where to vote tomorrow, go to the web site of the Bexar County Elections Deparment and you can find out where to vote based on your address.

Mike Thomas, fellow northwest San Antonio blogger, relates that his wife has nearly reached the point of exasperation due to the numerous automated phone calls they are receiving from both campaigns in recent days -- even threatening not to vote!

For some reason, our household hasn't been subject to this deluge of phone calls (perhaps because we voted over a week ago?). Still, I understand the nuisance of a phone repeatedly ringing, especially without any human beings to talk to. Yet this is a small price to pay to finally have the chance to cast a meaningful vote.

So many elections here recently have been essentially pre-determined, whether due to excessive gerrymandering or financial mismatch. Even the gubernatorial race where the winner got less than 40% was not particularly close. This congressional race, however, is a rare opportunity in Texas in recent years.

Let's make the most of it and get out to the polls to vote for Ciro Rodriguez, taking as many friends and family with us as we can. As President Clinton bluntly said yesterday, it's all up to us whether we in southwest Texas join the 29 districts that ousted Bush/DeLay-supporting Republicans or with the 10 that came up just short.

Meanwhile, if you want to hear and see more of yesterday's rally with Clinton and Ciro Rodriguez, here is another first-hand description from a campaign volunteer. Also, another local blogger I've recently discovered, Walker Report, has many photos of the event.

Photos from the Clinton GOTV rally for Ciro Rodriguez in San Antonio today

We attended the GOTV rally on the south side of San Antonio this afternoon to see President Clinton speak in support of Ciro Rodriguez in his race against GOP Congressman Henry Bonilla.  It was held in the gymnasium of Palo Alto College, and was a spirited and crowded event.

Performing were well-known Tejano musicians Los Texmaniacs and Johnny Canales, and speaking was a long list of prominent local Democratic politicians, from Congressman Charlie Gonzalez, to newly-elected state senator Carlos Uresti, to former mayor and former Clinton HUD secretary Henry Cisneros.  Uresti's speech was short and sweet, amounting to a brief cheerleading session.  Cisneros took a different approach with a rabble-rousing diatrabe against Henry Bonilla's treachery and in favor of Ciro Rodriguez's character.

The stars of the show were, of course, Ciro Rodriguez and President Bill Clinton, who entered the stage together.  Ciro introduced the former leader of the free world, who then gave typical strong performance.  Clinton's take-home message is that this election is going to be all about turnout--who wants it more.  He asked us in the crowd if we wanted to join the 29 districts who ousted Republicans in favor of Democrats, or if we would be join the list of 10 or 11 districts that came up just a percentage point short.  The crowd's answer was loud and clear.

Here are some other pictures I was able to get, from our fortunate vantage point in the front row, earned via a relatively early arrival and due to the phone banking work that my wife has been putting in over the last couple weeks.  I also have a couple brief videos that I will be putting up shortly (UPDATE: see here for the videos of Cisneros and Clinton).

December 09, 2006

Bill Clinton to stump for Ciro Rodriguez Sunday in San Antonio

The last day of early voting in Rodriguez-Bonilla race for Congress is today, as polls will remain open until 8 pm.  Early voting was originally supposed to end yesterday, but the controversy over the Republican-decided early election date of December 12 was resolved by a compromise to extend early voting an extra day into the weekend.  Tuesday is then the official election day, once and for all.

The race is obviously close.  The only public poll I've seen indicates a seven point lead for Bonilla, but the statistical margin of error on that poll is large, not to mention the difficulty in determining who the likely voters are in this unusual December election.

Henry Bonilla obviously feels he is in serious danger of losing his seat, and his prominent role in Congress as the GOP's only Mexican-American Hispanic.  He has resorted to the desperate and despicable tactic of labelling Ciro Rodriguez as a terrorist sympathizer in a major media push.  This charge is so ludicrous that it seems likely to backfire on Bonilla, but he obviously felt it worth the risk.  That indicates to me that he thinks this race is no better than even for him.

Now, tomorrow, two days before the big day, former President Bill Clinton is coming to town to stump for votes to help Rodriguez defeat Bonilla. 

Former President Bill Clinton will stump Sunday in San Antonio for Democrat Ciro Rodriguez, ratcheting up the profile of his runoff challenge to U.S. Rep. Henry Bonilla in Congressional District 23.

The president expressed an interest in coming out to support Ciro and to energize Democratic voters, said Vanessa Gonzalez, Rodriguez's spokeswoman.

She said Clinton would speak at a get-out-the-vote rally thats scheduled to start at 12:30 p.m. in the Palo Alto Colleges gymnasium on the South Side. Tickets, available after 3 p.m. today, are required for admission and can be obtained by contacting Rodriguezs campaign.

Bonilla's reaction?

Time after time, Bonilla said in a written statement, during this campaign Ciro Rodriguez has used questionable judgment, in this case serving as a cheerleader for a man who has an unwavering commitment to big government and the price tag that comes with it.

Given Clinton's record of budget surpluses, and the GOP's and Bush's record of massive deficit spending, once again Bonilla is resorting to ludicrous, reality-denying charges.  He does not deserve to remain in Congress as the representative of this district.

December 08, 2006

Battle over uncontrolled development in Kendall County

Are some Texas starting to wake up to the necessity to rein in uncontrolled development?  It is looking that way, since a fight is brewing in ultra-conservative Kendall County, in the Hill Country about 30 miles northwest of downtown San Antonio, and the victim of our city's rapidly expanding sea of sprawl.

Real estate developers, accustomed to being able to do as they please in this state, battle even the slightest hint of local control over their behavior.

Aside from my general interest in this subject, I have a personal interest in this area.  When we first moved to the San Antonio area in eight years ago, we rented a small house there, just south of Boerne (pronounced "bernie") for about 18 months, and we still live only a few miles away.  Our current area has recently been overrun by development, and Kendall County, much of which is still open ranchlands outside of the Boerne area , is next on the horizon.

From the San Antonio Express-News yesterday (emphasis added):

Kendall County officials say intense pressure from developers requires more local regulatory power to help preserve the very lifestyle that draws new residents there.

Two recent lawsuits underscore growing tensions between the county and developers, some of whom contend local leaders are overstepping their authority.

...

Since bills aimed at giving Texas counties greater power have faced perennial defeat in the Legislature, Commissioner Gene Miertschin asked Sen. Jeff Wentworth, R-San Antonio, and Rep.-elect Nathan Macias, R-New Braunfels, to carry a proposal that would apply only to Kendall County.

The bill, which is now being revised, would give county officials power over land use similar to zoning authority, letting them dictate the size, locations, dimensions and uses of buildings in unincorporated areas.

...

Boerne City Councilman Bob Manning said delaying action may prove costly.

"We are on the radar screen of the largest and most sophisticated developers in the nation," he said, quoting consultants hired to update the city's master plan. "We really do need to address the tidal wave of pressure that is coming at us from both east and west on Texas 46."

The county also faces development from the north, via Kerr County, where Greg Blume, builder of Falls Ranch subdivision, wants to tie in to Kendall County roads to reach Interstate 10 in Boerne.

Concerns about road degradation from construction traffic led Kendall commissioners in August to pass regulations that prohibit the use of its roads to access the subdivision.

But the builder ignored the rules, according to a lawsuit the county filed Nov. 16 against Blume, Btex Ranch and Edward Neil Development.

"Once again, a developer is challenging the county's authority to protect the health, safety and welfare of Kendall County's residents," said Allee, the county attorney.

Blume asserts that county leaders are overstepping their authority, and said, "I'm just looking forward to what the judge will say."

The outcome of this brewing battle northwest of here may well decide the fate of this stunning area of the Hill Country.  What will be left of it in 20 years?  In 50 years?

December 01, 2006

Early voting in Bexar County is on for this weekend

In Bexar County, early voting is back on for this weekend for the congressional runoff between Ciro Rodriguez and incumbent Henry Bonilla -- starting tomorrow.  Our local election officials are ignoring the demands of state GOP leaders to push back the start of early voting until Monday.  Once again, the GOP finds itself on the side of making it harder for people to vote.

Bexar County's elections chief said late Thursday that early voting for the runoff in Congressional District 23 is back on for Saturday a move flouting the Texas secretary of state.

Bexar County commissioners earlier this week voted unanimously to start early voting Saturday, adding two days to the voting schedule set by Gov. Rick Perry. Their vote came in response to petitions filed by Democrat Ciro Rodriguez's campaign and the League of United Latin American Citizens.

However, the secretary of state's office challenged the county's authority to add days, prompting Bexar County Elections Administrator Jacque Callanen to withdraw her plan Wednesday and revert to the schedule set by Perry, which had early voting beginning Monday.

Callanen reversed herself Thursday.

"I believe it was the right thing to do when they submitted the petition," she said. "I think (the authority) ultimately resides in my office, and we're going to honor the petition."

When told of Callanen's move, Scott Haywood, a spokesman for the secretary of state's office, said, "The election code and state law don't give the county the authority to do that."

So it looks like we're going to be able to vote for Ciro Rodriguez as early as tomorrow morning.
